### **The Problem with "One Cup" of Flour**

Here is the dirty secret of baking: **"One cup" is not a standard unit of measurement when it comes to solids.**

Depending on how you pack the flour, "one cup" can weigh anywhere from **120 grams** (if sifted) to **150 grams** (if scooped directly from the bag). That is a **25% difference** in the most important ingredient in your recipe! 

*   **Too much flour:** Dry, crumbly cakes and hard cookies.
*   **Too little flour:** Flat, spreading cookies and collapsing cakes.

This is why converting your recipes from volume (cups) to weight (grams) is the instant upgrade your baking needs.

### **Liquid vs. Dry Ounces: Don't Get Fooled**

One of the most confusing parts of conversion is the word "ounce." 
*   **Fluid Ounces (fl oz)** measure **volume** (space).
*   **Dry Ounces (oz)** measure **weight** (mass).

For water, they are the same (8 fl oz of water weighs 8 oz). But for almost everything else, they are different. **1 cup of flour is 8 fluid ounces of space, but it only weighs 4.4 ounces.** If you weigh out 8 ounces of flour for a recipe calling for "1 cup," you will ruin your dough.

*Always double-check if your recipe asks for fluid ounces (volume) or weight ounces.*

### **Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)**

**Q: Can I use a liquid measuring cup for flour?**
A: No! Liquid measuring cups are designed to be filled to a line below the rim. Dry measuring cups are designed to be filled to the brim and leveled off. Using the wrong cup can throw off your measurement by 10-15%.

**Q: How many grams is a stick of butter?**
A: In the US, a standard stick of butter is 1/2 cup or **113 grams**.

**Q: Why do professional bakers use grams?**
A: Grams are much smaller units than ounces, allowing for higher precision. It’s easier to add "5 grams" of salt than "0.17 ounces."
